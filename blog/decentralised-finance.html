DeFi

20th February 2021

Around the time I left for France, the Bank of England dropped interest rates to 0.5%. I also heard some rumours that negative interest rates may be implemented in the near future to encourage the public to spend their money, instead of saving it.

In theory, negative interest rates mean you'd be paying the banks a small amount for holding onto your money for you.


What is DeFi?

Decentralised finance is a relatively new concept. DeFi is a blanket term for financial services with no central authority. Nobody in charge. It's a system that uses protocols, smart contracts and decentralised applications (dApps) together, usually on the Ethereum blockchain, to create a financial system that is free and open to anyone with an internet connection.

Traditional banks are beginning to show their age. Opening a bank account is a very regulated process. It could take weeks, multiple appointments and a lot of paperwork depending on your geographical location.

DeFi aims to be the opposite of the above - open-source, permissionless, transparent, censorship resistant, as well as giving you full authority over your money.


DeFi Lending

I tried to get a bank loan a few years back but didn't get accepted. Maybe I was too young. Maybe they'd performed a credit check on me and thought I wouldn't to be able to pay it back.

Decentralised loans work without an intermediary. They don't require an age or credit check, just some collateral that's locked into a smart contract, meaning that if you don't repay the loan, the collateral will be taken.

The lending and borrowing rates of almost all defi protocols are much better than traditional banks, because nobody is using your money to make money. The lenders are receiving almost all the interest from borrowers.
